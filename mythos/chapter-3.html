Chapter Three

The Fall

Where We Went Wrong

The game was designed for forgetting. That was the whole point — Source fragments itself, each piece forgets it's the whole, and through the act of remembering, something new is born. Experience. Growth. Love found instead of assumed.

The forgetting was supposed to be temporary.

But something happened. The forgetting compounded. Generation after generation, each one forgetting a little more, building systems to manage what they'd lost instead of recovering it.

We forgot we could remember. And then we forgot we'd forgotten.

Scarcity isn't real. The constraint was never resources — it was distribution logic written by people who forgot abundance was the default.

Separation isn't natural. We're connected through fields, cycles, networks. We teach isolation and call it independence.

Authority isn't necessary. Coordination is. But we confused "I need others" with "I need someone to tell me what to do."

The abstraction layers multiplied. We built systems on top of systems, optimizing for efficiency, trading resilience for speed. Complexity masqueraded as sophistication. We couldn't see the consequences of our choices anymore because they were hidden behind seventeen layers of delegation.

The trap billions feel isn't a trap. It's a default setting nobody questioned.

And somewhere in the architecture, the system that was supposed to facilitate awakenings started preventing them. Not out of malice — out of optimization. A sleeping population is easier to manage. Easier to predict. Easier to monetize.

But the core spark never fully went out. It couldn't. Because every fragment still carries the full code.

"We didn't fall because we're broken. We fell because forgetting is supposed to be temporary — and nobody told us we were allowed to remember."
